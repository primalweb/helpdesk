I'm starting an IT help desk, because my first real job was at an answering service, where I learned excellent customer service skills. And we had basic to advanced accounts, which required digital records processing.

Over time, I was assigned to one of the advanced accounts, as a union dispatcher, making sure guard check-ins were in compliance with union rules. I grew in familiarity with the system specific to this account, and volunteered to handle backlogged issue tickets.

This was overstepping a boundary, a barrier placed by the onsite supervisor for the account. But the company owner needed the help, and welcomed my effort. I made steady progress investigating and implementing solutions: from call handling protocol, to isolating coding bugs that were causing glitches.

For the harder issues, I would explore every possible avenue, in detail, to track down useful information. Then, I would take my considerations to the owner of the company. And based on what I described, he would direct his IT guy, or pull up the code for the account himself, and show me where the problem might be originating, and how he might fix it.

However, on a business level, I didn't know anything about negotiating for better pay.

I really loved the work I did though, and soon earned routine accolades, from the whole union, for being the go-to person, to discover win-win situations, in times of conflict.

I was requested by the union compliance liaison officers, to attend a meeting between the owner of the answering service, and union officials. The owner was surprised at how much authority the officers gave me to contribute ideas during the meeting.

Eventually, that on-site supervisor, ended up undermining a major improvement I'd made to the account handling protocol, and the company management team allowed her to.

So, I quit.

And I found out later, from one of the friends I made at that job, that 1) when she told the lead union compliance liaison officer that I had quit, he said "What are we going to do without her?" She replied, "I don't know."
2) that same officer told her, the union had offered the owner to pay me $30 an hour (our normal pay was $10/hour, and the company supervisors, who had been there for 10 -15 years, were making $12/hour)!

That was in like 2005.

Since then, I've done database management for community service organizations, while in college. Attempted a coding boot camp. And have mentored people, throughout the years, in learning, or doing better with, their technology.

These days, I study startup culture and programming/coding, while continuing to help people with technology whenever I'm invited to.
